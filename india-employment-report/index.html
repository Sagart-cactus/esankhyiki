01

Unemployment Rate Trend

The Unemployment Rate (UR) under the usual status (PS+SS) approach measures the percentage of the labour force that is jobless. India’s UR has declined steadily from 6% in 2017–18 to 3.2% in 2023–24, with male and female rates converging.

Unemployment Rate — Person, Male & Female (PS+SS, Combined)

Key insight: Female unemployment has fallen faster than male — from 5.6% to 3.2% — and is now equal to the male rate. The gender gap in unemployment has effectively closed by 2023–24.
02

Labour Force Participation Rate

The LFPR measures the share of the working-age population (15+) that is either employed or actively seeking work. India’s LFPR has risen sharply from 49.8% to 60.1%, driven almost entirely by a surge in female participation.

Labour Force Participation Rate — by Gender (PS+SS, Combined)

Key insight: Female LFPR nearly doubled from 23.3% to 41.7% in 7 years, while male LFPR rose modestly from 75.8% to 78.8%. Women entering the workforce is the single biggest structural shift in India’s labour market.
03

Worker Population Ratio

WPR indicates the proportion of the population that is actually employed. Unlike LFPR, it excludes those seeking work. India’s WPR has risen from 46.8% to 58.2%, indicating that more people are finding and holding jobs.

Worker Population Ratio — by Gender (PS+SS, Combined)

Key insight: Female WPR jumped from 22.0% to 40.3% — an 83% increase. Male WPR also improved from 71.2% to 76.3%, reflecting broad-based employment gains across genders.
04

Youth vs Overall Unemployment

Youth (15–29 age group) unemployment is a critical indicator. While it has fallen significantly from 17.8% to 10.2%, it remains roughly 3 times the overall rate — a structural challenge that reflects skill mismatches and the “educated unemployment” paradox.

Youth (15–29) vs Overall Unemployment Rate (%)

Key insight: Youth UR dropped 43% from 17.8% to 10.2%, but the ratio to overall UR has stayed persistently at ~3x. Young Indians still face disproportionately high joblessness despite the overall improvement.
05

Rural vs Urban Unemployment

Rural and urban labour markets differ significantly. Rural India has consistently lower unemployment due to agriculture and informal work absorption. Urban unemployment remains more than double the rural rate, reflecting formal sector job scarcity.

Rural vs Urban Unemployment Rate (PS+SS)

Key insight: Rural UR fell to 2.4% in 2023–24 while urban remained at 5.4%. The urban-rural gap has widened from 2.4pp to 3.0pp, suggesting urban job creation hasn’t kept pace with rural labour absorption.
06

Gender Gap in LFPR

The gender gap in labour force participation has been India’s biggest structural weakness. While the gap has narrowed from 52.5pp to 37.1pp, men still participate at nearly double the rate of women. The trend, however, is decisively positive.

Male vs Female LFPR — Grouped Comparison

Key insight: The male-female LFPR gap shrank from 52.5 percentage points in 2017–18 to 37.1pp in 2023–24. At this rate of closure (~2.2pp/year), parity would still take 15+ years.
07

Education & Unemployment

India exhibits a striking paradox: higher education correlates with higher unemployment. Graduates face 13.4% unemployment while illiterates are at 0.5%. This reflects aspirational job-seeking among the educated and easier absorption of unskilled labour in the informal sector.

Unemployment Rate by Education Level — 2023–24 (%)

Key insight: Diploma holders (9.2%) and graduates (13.4%) have the highest unemployment. The “educated unemployment” problem persists because formal sector job creation lags behind the supply of degree holders.
08

Worker Distribution by Status

India’s workforce is dominated by self-employment (57.3%), reflecting the large informal economy. Regular salaried employment — the most “formal” category — accounts for just 20.9%, while casual labour makes up 21.8%.

Employment Status Distribution — 2023–24 (%)

Key insight: Over half of India’s workers are self-employed, indicating the economy is still heavily informal. The share of regular/salaried jobs has remained stubbornly around 21–23%, underscoring the challenge of formal job creation.
